## 🎯 This Week's Signal

**The "dark factory" moment for knowledge work is closer than the consensus thinks — and the window to position ahead of it is measured in months, not years.**

Lenny Rachitsky's interview this week with Anthropic's Amol Avasare documents Claude going from $1B to $19B ARR — the fastest growth of any AI product in history. That number is important not because it tells you about Anthropic's business, but because of what it reveals about adoption velocity. Separately, Lenny's "AI state of the union" piece this week explicitly uses the term "dark factories" — fully automated production environments with no humans on the floor — and maps that concept directly onto knowledge work. The argument: we've passed the inflection point where AI can do meaningful *work*, not just assist with it. The remaining question is how fast org structures, pricing, and market expectations catch up to the capability curve.

What makes this week's signal different from generic "AI is coming" noise: the GitHub Trending data corroborates it at the practitioner layer. **oh-my-claudecode** (7.5k stars this week) is a teams-first multi-agent orchestration layer for Claude Code. **oh-my-codex** (14k stars) adds hooks, agent teams, and HUDs to OpenAI's Codex. **claude-howto** (10.7k stars) is a visual, example-driven guide to Claude Code for practitioners. Three of the top eight trending repos this week are explicitly about *orchestrating* AI coding agents — not using them, orchestrating them. That's the tell. The market has moved from "can I use AI to write code?" to "how do I run a team of AI agents?" in approximately one product cycle.

The 12-month implication for Justin: the solopreneur who figures out multi-agent orchestration for a specific vertical this year will have the structural cost advantage that offshore arbitrage gave businesses in the 2000s — except it compounds. The window to build that expertise before it's commodified is the next two quarters. The opportunity isn't building another AI wrapper; it's being the person who *runs* AI agent teams for a niche that doesn't have internal capability yet.

## 🔗 Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis

Here's the non-obvious connection this week: **the "salaryman vs. specialist" debate that Noah Smith is having about labour economics is structurally identical to the "monolith vs. microservices" debate that software engineers had in 2015** — and it resolves the same way.

Noah Smith's piece "Salarymen, specialists, and small businesses" (Apr 3) argues that AI may tilt the labour market toward generalists — people who can coordinate across functions — rather than deep specialists, because AI handles the specialist execution. This mirrors the microservices moment: when cloud infrastructure made it cheap to run distributed systems, the premium shifted from "person who can write optimised C++" to "person who can architect systems across many services."

The business model implication is direct: the solopreneur who builds AI-native workflows isn't trying to be a specialist anymore. They're running a distributed system of AI agents, each handling a specialist function, while the human provides the coordination layer — client relationships, judgment calls, context-setting. This is exactly what oh-my-claudecode is building at the tooling layer: "teams-first multi-agent orchestration." The software and the labour economics are converging on the same architecture.

For Justin: the $500k–$3M ARR business isn't built by being the best at one thing anymore. It's built by being the best *orchestrator* of AI agents in a specific vertical. The moat is the workflow design, the prompt architecture, and the client relationship — not the model itself.

This also explains why the Meridian ($17M raise for "agentic spreadsheets") funding is interesting: spreadsheets are already the world's most popular coordination tool. Making them agentic is literally turning a coordination layer into an orchestration system. The VC thesis and the labour economics thesis are the same thesis.

## 💡 Contrarian Take

**The consensus says "AI agents will replace knowledge workers." The more accurate and more useful frame is: "AI agents will replace knowledge work *firms*, not knowledge workers."**

Everyone is modelling this as individual displacement — your job gets automated. But look at what's actually happening in the GitHub Trending data this week: teams of AI agents, orchestrated by a single human. That's not replacing a worker; it's replacing a *company*. A solo operator running oh-my-claudecode with five specialised agents isn't replacing one developer — they're replacing a five-person development shop.

The economic consequence is that the disruption won't show up as mass unemployment in the near term. It'll show up as pricing collapse in professional services markets — the $20k/month agency retainer becomes $3k/month from a one-person AI-native shop that delivers equivalent output. Employment numbers stay relatively stable (per Gary Marcus's "don't panic yet" piece from Apr 1) while revenue concentration shifts violently toward the orchestrators and away from the traditional service firms.

For Justin, this is the actionable reframe: he's not trying to build a software company that competes with Anthropic or OpenAI. He's trying to become the best one-person professional services firm in a specific vertical — one that uses AI agents as staff. The competition isn't other AI startups; it's incumbent agencies and consultancies who haven't restructured yet.
